Focus Question 1

What are the main philosophical formulations of the problem of evil?

What Is the Problem of Evil?

The problem of evil is one of the oldest and most persistent challenges in the philosophy of religion. At its core, it asks how an all-powerful, all-knowing, and perfectly good God could allow evil and suffering to exist in the world. As philosopher Michael Tooley explains, the problem arises from an apparent contradiction between the attributes traditionally ascribed to God and the observable reality of the world (Tooley). Scholars have developed several distinct philosophical formulations of this problem, each posing a different kind of challenge to theistic belief (Trakakis, The Problem of Evil: Eight Views in Dialogue).

The Logical Problem of Evil

The logical problem of evil, most famously articulated by J.L. Mackie, argues that the existence of God and the existence of evil are logically incompatible. If God is omnipotent, He has the power to eliminate evil. If He is omnibenevolent, He has the desire to do so. And yet evil plainly exists — which, under this formulation, makes the existence of such a God logically impossible (Trakakis, "The Evidential Problem of Evil"). This is sometimes called the "deductive" or "incompatibility" argument, and it sets a high bar: it does not merely claim that evil is evidence against God, but that the two are flatly contradictory (Meister and Cary).

The Evidential Problem of Evil

Later philosophers, most notably William Rowe, shifted the argument. Rather than claiming that evil makes God's existence logically impossible, the evidential problem of evil holds that the sheer amount and intensity of suffering in the world makes God's existence improbable. The question is not whether God and evil can coexist in principle, but whether a perfectly good God would permit the particular kinds and quantities of suffering we actually observe — especially innocent suffering that seems to serve no greater purpose (Tooley). This "probabilistic" or "inductive" formulation is considered by many contemporary philosophers to be the more compelling challenge to theism (Trakakis, "The Evidential Problem of Evil").

Moral Evil vs. Natural Evil

Both formulations depend on a foundational distinction between two types of evil. Moral evil refers to suffering caused by the free choices of human beings — war, cruelty, murder, and injustice. Natural evil refers to suffering arising from the natural world itself — disease, earthquakes, famine, and death independent of human action. This distinction matters because different theodicies (defenses of God's goodness in the face of evil) apply to each type differently (Meister and Cary). For instance, free will arguments may account for moral evil but leave natural evil largely unexplained (Trakakis, The Problem of Evil: Eight Views in Dialogue).

Contemporary Voices

These philosophical formulations are not confined to academic texts. Public discourse around the problem of evil reflects the same distinctions scholars debate. In a widely shared Facebook post, Atta Ur Rehman surveys Bertrand Russell's objections to theism and outlines the logical, evidential, and experiential dimensions of the problem of evil — demonstrating that these questions remain active in contemporary conversation (Rehman).
