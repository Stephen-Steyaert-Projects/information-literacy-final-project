Focus Question 2

How do major theodicies attempt to reconcile God's existence with evil?

What Is a Theodicy?

A theodicy is a philosophical or theological defense of God's goodness and justice in the face of evil and suffering. The term comes from the Greek theos (God) and dike (justice) — literally, a justification of God. Rather than denying the reality of evil, theodicies attempt to explain why a perfectly good and all-powerful God would permit it. Christian thinkers have proposed several major theodicies, each drawing on different theological and philosophical foundations (Meister and Cary). These approaches are not always mutually exclusive — many Christian theologians combine elements of more than one (Jaiprakash).

The Free Will Defense

The free will defense is among the most widely held Christian responses to the problem of moral evil. It holds that God granted humans genuine freedom of will, and that a world containing free creatures capable of love, virtue, and relationship with God is more valuable than a world of morally determined beings — even if that freedom is sometimes misused to cause harm. Evil, under this view, is not God's creation but the result of human choices made against God's will (Meister and Cary). Ronnie Campbell's Trinitarian theodicy extends this argument, grounding the possibility of free, loving relationship in the very nature of the Trinity — a God who is eternally relational cannot coerce created persons without undermining the love He seeks (Campbell).

Augustinian Theodicy

Augustine of Hippo argued that evil has no independent existence — it is not a thing God created, but a privation of good (privatio boni). Just as darkness is the absence of light, evil is the absence or corruption of goodness. In Augustine's framework, God created everything good — as Scripture affirms, "God saw all that He had made, and behold, it was very good" (Gen. 1.31, NASB1995). Evil entered the world through the free choices of angels and then of humanity in the Fall (Gen. 3). Original sin is thus the root of both moral evil (human wickedness) and natural evil (suffering and death in the physical world) (Jaiprakash). This theodicy places responsibility squarely on created beings rather than on God, preserving divine goodness while acknowledging the depth of human fallenness (Sanguineti).

Soul-Making Theodicy (Irenaean)

The soul-making theodicy, associated with the early church father Irenaeus and developed in the modern era by John Hick, takes a different approach. Rather than explaining evil as the result of a fall from an original perfection, it argues that humans were never created perfect — we are made in the image of God but are called to grow into the likeness of God through experience, struggle, and moral development. On this view, evil and suffering are not simply permitted by God as a consequence of sin; they are the necessary conditions for genuine moral and spiritual growth (Meister and Cary). A world of comfort and ease could not produce courage, compassion, or faith. Matthew Ramage's examination of evolutionary suffering engages this tradition, exploring how natural processes — including predation and death — might be understood within an Augustinian and Thomistic framework as part of God's providential ordering of creation toward a greater end (Ramage).

The Thomistic Approach

Thomas Aquinas synthesizes and deepens the Augustinian tradition (Sanguineti). He affirms that God is not the cause of evil but permits it insofar as He can draw greater goods from it — a principle rooted in His providential governance of creation. For Aquinas, physical evils (pain, death, natural disaster) are not intrinsically bad in an absolute sense but are deficiencies relative to a particular nature; they are ordered by God toward the perfection of the whole (Sanguineti). Aquinas also distinguishes carefully between God's antecedent will (that all be saved and that no evil occur) and His consequent will (which permits evil in light of human freedom and the ordering of creation). This framework allows Aquinas to maintain both divine omnipotence and genuine human responsibility (Ramage).
