Focus Question 3

What biblical passages or theological principles do Christians use to address evil and suffering?

Scripture as a Framework for Suffering

Christian engagement with evil and suffering is not limited to philosophical argument. Scripture itself provides the primary framework through which believers interpret and endure suffering — not by explaining it away, but by situating it within a larger story of creation, fall, redemption, and restoration. Bosman and van Wieringen observe that the biblical tradition, particularly the Book of Job, offers resources for confronting suffering that secular theodicy cannot — a God who is present in suffering, not merely a logician's abstraction (Bosman and van Wieringen).

The Book of Job

The Book of Job is Scripture's most sustained and unflinching engagement with innocent suffering. Job is a righteous man who loses everything — his wealth, his children, his health — through no fault of his own. He does not accept easy answers. He cries out, questions, and demands a response from God. When God finally speaks, He does not offer a philosophical explanation; He reveals His own sovereign majesty: "Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?" (Job 38.4, NASB1995). The answer Job receives is not an argument but an encounter — a reminder that God's wisdom and governance of creation surpass human comprehension. Bosman and van Wieringen draw on this passage to argue that Job models a theodicy of presence and trust rather than intellectual resolution (Bosman and van Wieringen).

Romans 8:28 and Providential Hope

Perhaps no verse is more frequently cited by Christians in the face of suffering than Romans 8:28: "And we know that God causes all things to work together for good to those who love God, to those who are called according to His purpose" (Rom. 8.28, NASB1995). This is not an assertion that suffering is painless or that evil is illusory — it is a declaration of God's redemptive sovereignty. All things, including tragedy and loss, are held within His providential purpose. Crystal Park and her colleagues find that this kind of belief — that suffering has ultimate meaning within God's plan — is central to how many Christians psychologically process what they call "theodical struggling," the inner conflict that arises when suffering challenges one's beliefs about God's goodness and power (Park et al.).

Lamentations and Honest Grief

The Christian tradition does not demand cheerful acceptance of suffering. The book of Lamentations models raw, unfiltered grief before God: "Is it nothing to all you who pass by? Look and see if there is any pain like my pain" (Lam. 1.12, NASB1995). Lament is itself a theological act — it presupposes that God is present and worth addressing, even in desolation. Tate Williams argues that the problem of evil has a deeply interpersonal dimension: suffering damages not just one's intellectual beliefs but one's personal relationship with God, and the biblical tradition of lament is one of the primary ways that relationship is sustained through pain (Williams). Bosman and van Wieringen similarly note that honest grief, modeled in Scripture, is part of how Christians maintain faith amid catastrophe (Bosman and van Wieringen).

Free Will, Sovereignty, and Compatibilism

A key theological principle underlying the Christian response to evil is the compatibility of human freedom with divine sovereignty — the view that God's providential governance of creation does not eliminate genuine human agency. M. Barnwell's analysis of soul-making theodicy engages this question through the lens of compatibilism: the idea that a meaningful kind of free will is compatible with God's ordering of events toward good ends (Barnwell). This matters for theodicy because it allows Christians to affirm simultaneously that humans are genuinely responsible for moral evil and that God is ultimately working all things toward redemption — without collapsing into fatalism or excusing wrongdoing. The theological principle is not that God causes evil, but that He is sovereign over it.
